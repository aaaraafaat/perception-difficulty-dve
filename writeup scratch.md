Why does a low or high DCP score mean low or high fog?It doesn't measure fog directly. It measures one thing: how dark the darkest parts of the image are. The reasoning chain that connects that to fog is:A normal clear photo always contains some genuinely dark spots — shadows, dark objects, dark colours. Fog is a pale veil of scattered light laid over everything; it lifts those dark spots toward grey. So: dark spots survive → probably clear → low score. Dark spots erased → probably fog → high score. The score is a proxy — it never sees fog, it infers fog from missing darkness. That inference is only as good as the assumption behind it.

This is the most important thing to be clear about, and the honest answer is: no, not as a fog meter. DCP (the dark channel prior, He et al. 2009) was invented to remove haze from images — to make foggy photos look clearer. It rests on an observation that holds for most everyday outdoor daytime photos: in such images, most small patches contain at least one very dark pixel. That observation is a statistical regularity, not a law. People later borrowed it as a rough fog-strength score because it's cheap and often correlates with haze. But it was never proven to measure fog, and it inherits a hard limit: the moment an image has low pixels for a reason other than clear air — night, coloured light, dark scenes — the prior is violated and the score lies. So in your thesis, DCP is "a widely-used physics-based proxy whose assumption fails under specific conditions," not "a fog sensor." That framing is the honest one, and it's also exactly what your project is about

Why does DCP call dark/night scenes "clear"?
Because it can't tell why a pixel is dark. A dark pixel can be dark because the air is clear (you're seeing a real dark object), or because there's no light on it (night, shadow, unlit building). DCP assumes the first and ignores the second. A foggy night is full of genuinely dark regions (the unlit sky, dark buildings), so its darkest-pixel measure stays low — and DCP reports "clear" even though the air is thick with haze. The darkness is real; it just means the opposite of what the cue assumes. And you're right that we've only eyeballed one or two — there could be many low-light scenes doing this, which is exactly why looking at a larger sorted set is the correct next move (more below).

Grey things fooling DCP — is there a property that explains it?
Yes, and you remembering this from the base thesis is a good catch. There are two distinct grey traps, and they're worth separating:
The first is bright grey — pale grey sky, white walls, washed-out grey surfaces. Pale grey means all three colour channels are high together, so there are no dark pixels there, so the dark channel reads high → "fog" — even on a clear day. This is the bright-sky failure.
The second is the deeper one, and it's the property underneath your whole investigation: the dark channel responds to greyness vs. colourfulness, not really to fog. Fog happens to make scenes pale and grey, which is why DCP works sometimes. But anything that makes a scene grey — overcast flat light, grey roads, grey concrete, distant grey haze — pushes the score the same way, and anything strongly coloured (the orange lights) pushes it the other way regardless of actual fog. So the single property that explains most DCP failures is: DCP is really a "how grey / how washed-out is this image" detector, which we are hoping is a fog detector — and they only agree when greyness is caused by fog and nothing else. Night breaks it (dark, not grey), coloured light breaks it (vivid, not grey), and grey-but-clear scenes break it (grey, but not from fog). That one sentence is, in plain terms, the core finding your thesis is circling.
